It's How You Say It

A quality-control desk for transcreation — built to protect the craft, not replace the people who do it.

Drafted by Wolf
What this is, in one breath

Not a translation button. Not a robot that replaces your transcreators. It's a quality-control desk: it takes a first pass, audits that pass against the source the way a careful reviewer would, flags only what actually deserves a human's attention, and — most important — remembers every correction your team makes so the house's judgment compounds instead of living in one or two people's heads.

You already say it better than anyone: it's not what you say, it's how you say it. This tool is built around that sentence, not against it.

I

The problem worth solving

Mendoza's edge isn't translation — it's transcreation. The difference is the whole business: a faithful rendering that lands flat in the target culture is, in your own words, just a collection of words and symbols. The judgment that turns words into resonance lives in experienced people: which phrasing reads warm versus textbook, which term sounds cheap, what plays in Mexican Spanish versus Caribbean versus U.S. Hispanic, where a brand's voice would bend or break.

That judgment is the asset. It's also the risk. It's mostly tacit, it's concentrated in a few reviewers, and the review work itself is repetitive enough that good people don't want to do it forever. If a key reviewer steps back, a real piece of the agency's standard can walk out the door with them — quietly, with no record of why the calls were made the way they were.

A tool can't replace that judgment. But it can do the grunt work underneath it, and it can capture the judgment as it's exercised — so the standard becomes a thing the agency owns, not a thing it rents from whoever happens to still be reviewing.

II

The one idea that makes it useful

Most review tools fail the same way: they either rubber-stamp everything ("looks good!") or they bury you in nitpicks until you stop reading them. Both are useless, for the same underlying reason — they don't separate two completely different things.

A piece of copy can almost always be polished. That does not mean the current version is wrong. The single most important thing this tool does is hold those two ideas apart and never confuse them:

"Acceptable, but could be sharper" is a different verdict from "this is wrong and needs fixing." The tool never lets the first masquerade as the second.

That distinction is what keeps it honest. Optional polish gets labeled as optional and stays out of your reviewers' way. Real problems — meaning changed, tone reversed, a number wrong — get surfaced loudly. Your people spend their attention only where judgment is actually required.

III

What it promises

Five commitments. They aren't slogans — each one is a rule the tool has to obey on every single page, the way your team has standards it won't break.

It serves transcreation, not just translation. Accuracy is the floor, not the goal.
It never rubber-stamps — and never buries you in trivia.
Numbers, names, dates, and dollar figures are checked exactly, every time.
It defends the work: a second, independent read audits the first.
Every human correction becomes house style. The judgment compounds.

If a feature would violate one of these, it doesn't ship. That's what keeps the tool from drifting into a generic "AI translator" that nobody on your team would trust with a client's voice.

IV

How a page moves through it

Plain version of the workflow. Source copy comes in. The tool produces a first-pass transcreation using the client's brief — target language, region, tone, audience, the brand glossary, and the do-not-translate list. Then a separate, independent pass compares that draft against the source and writes up specific observations. It's deliberately not allowed to grade its own homework, and it's not allowed to shrug and say "looks fine" — it has to actually scrutinize and produce findings.

Each finding comes back as a small card a reviewer can read in seconds:

Required fix · Tone SEVERITY 7
Source "An affordable plan for every family."
Draft "Un plan barato para cada familia."
Issue barato reads as cheap / low-quality in marketing copy — wrong emotional register for a value message.
Suggested "Un plan accesible para cada familia."

The reviewer accepts, edits, or overrides. Their final call — especially when they override — is the gold the tool is really after. That correction doesn't just fix this page; it feeds the knowledge base and quietly raises the floor on every page after it.

V

A severity scale that means something

Numbers from one to ten look precise and aren't — nobody can reliably tell a "5" from a "6." So the scale collapses to four bands, and each band maps to an actual decision. The number behind it is only there to sort within a band.

PolishPure preference. Logged, never shown unless asked. The draft was fine.
ImproveA genuinely better option exists. Offered, not required. The reviewer decides.
FixWrong, unclear, off-tone, or off-brand. Goes back for revision before it ships.
EscalateReversed meaning, a wrong figure, an omission, anything legally or culturally risky. A human looks, always.

The reviewer's queue only fills with the bottom two bands. The top two stay available but out of the way — there if someone wants the extra polish, invisible if they don't. That's the difference between a tool people use and a tool people mute.

VI

What it checks exactly — no opinion involved

Some errors aren't a matter of taste, and the tool shouldn't treat them like they are. Numbers, dates, names, phone numbers, addresses, and dollar amounts either match the source or they don't. These are the errors with legal and financial teeth — a "1,500" that became "15,000," a benefit date that shifted, a company name misspelled in a headline.

For those, the tool doesn't ask anyone's judgment. It compares them directly, character by character, and flags any mismatch automatically — every time, on every page, regardless of how clean the rest of the copy looks. The same goes for anything dropped from or added to the source. Taste is for humans; arithmetic is for the machine.

VII

The knowledge base — the actual asset

Everything above is useful. This is the part that's valuable. The first-pass-and-review loop is a convenience; the knowledge base is the agency's transcreation judgment, written down, owned, and growing. It's the answer to "what happens if our best reviewer retires" — the answer becomes "nothing, because their standards are in the system."

Every correction a reviewer makes can become a reusable house rule, in a form anyone can read:

Avoid barato
Prefer económico · accesible
Why barato can sound cheap or low-quality in marketing copy.
Approved M. Mendoza · for value & affordability messaging

Over time the base holds the things that make Mendoza's work Mendoza's: preferred and forbidden terms, the do-not-translate list (client names, product names, slogans) versus what should be transcreated, regional voice notes (warm and clear for U.S. Hispanic marketing, not textbook-formal), per-client brand-voice rules with real examples and counterexamples, and a record of every approved final correction. New copy automatically arrives pre-loaded with the rules that apply to it, so the same call never has to be made twice.

The first version of this tool is a helpful assistant. The hundredth version is the agency's institutional memory — and it got there one human correction at a time.

VIII

Where we'd start small

The mistake here would be to build all of that at once. The right first version is deliberately tiny, and proves the loop before anything fancy gets added. It would do exactly this and no more:

  • Paste in source copy and pick a direction (English ↔ Spanish), a region, a tone, and the client.
  • Generate a first-pass transcreation.
  • Run the independent review and surface the observations, each with its band and suggested fix.
  • Flag the exact-match problems — numbers, names, dates — automatically.
  • Let a reviewer accept or edit the final version.
  • Save that final correction into the knowledge base.

That's the whole engine. File upload, page-by-page processing, side-by-side diffs, client dashboards, per-campaign style guides — all of that is real and all of it can come later. None of it matters until the small loop earns its keep. Build the engine first; decorate it once it's proven.

IX

What it is, finally

It is not a ChatGPT wrapper, though it uses a model to draft. It is not an auto-translator, though it translates. It is not a replacement for your transcreators, though it does work they'd recognize. Calling it any of those misses the point.

It's a quality-control desk that does the repetitive part, refuses to cry wolf, checks the facts exactly, and turns your team's judgment into something the agency permanently owns.

The value was never the translation button — that part is a commodity now, and Mendoza was never in the commodity business. The value is the workflow around it and the memory underneath it: consistent standards, a review that's honest about what actually needs a human, and a knowledge base that makes the agency's transcreation judgment more durable every month instead of more fragile. That's a tool worth building, and it's built squarely around the thing you already do better than anyone.
