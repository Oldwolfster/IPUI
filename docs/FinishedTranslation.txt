<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">I</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The <em>diagnosis</em></h2>

  <p>Neural networks have a three-stroke engine. Guess, check, nudge. Take an input, compute a prediction, measure how wrong you were, adjust the parameters slightly in the direction of less wrong. Repeat. That's all gradient descent is, mechanically. That's all backpropagation is, philosophically — the same operation applied with a credit-assignment story so each parameter knows how much to nudge.</p>

  <p>The engine is simple. The cult around the engine is not.</p>

  <p>A modern practitioner working in PyTorch is asked to know — or to "select sensible values for" — at least the following: learning rate, batch size, optimizer family (SGD, Momentum, Adam, AdamW, RAdam, Nadam, AdaGrad, RMSprop), per-optimizer hyperparameters (β₁, β₂, ε, ρ, decay schedules), loss function (MSE, MAE, BCE, CCE, Huber, Hinge, Log-Cosh, Focal, Wasserstein), weight initialization (Xavier, He, Lecun, Orthogonal, sparse), activation function per layer (ReLU, LeakyReLU, ELU, GELU, SELU, Swish, Mish, tanh, sigmoid, softmax, linear), input scaling, target scaling, regularization (L1, L2, ElasticNet, dropout, batch normalization, layer normalization, weight decay), architecture (depth, width, skip connections, residual blocks), training schedule, and gradient handling.</p>

  <p>Each of these dials has a defensible reason to exist and, for most users on most problems, exactly one reasonable setting. The cost of this asymmetry — many dials, few meaningful choices — is that every newcomer to neural networks spends months learning to ignore the dials they shouldn't have been given in the first place.</p>

  <p>The industry's response to this complexity has been to write more documentation. NeuroForge's response is different.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">II</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The four <em>Sacred Laws</em></h2>

  <p>NeuroForge is governed by four declarative commitments. They are not "principles" — that word is too soft. They are constraints on every feature, every UI element, every code path.</p>

  <div class="sacred-laws">
    <div class="law"><strong>100% Auditable.</strong> <em>Absolutely everything.</em></div>
    <div class="law"><strong>200% Deterministic.</strong> <em>Absolutely everything.</em></div>
    <div class="law"><strong>User responsible for</strong> <em>absolutely nothing.</em></div>
    <div class="law"><strong>But able to override</strong> <em>absolutely everything.</em></div>
  </div>

  <p>These are operationally precise. They tell you, in a given design dilemma, what is acceptable and what is not. Not "we strive for transparency" — <em>everything must be observable</em>. Not "we provide good defaults" — <em>the user is responsible for nothing</em>. Not "expert users can configure" — <em>every default is overridable</em>.</p>

  <p>Most ML tooling violates at least one of these laws on most days. Auto-tuned hyperparameters live in inaccessible state. RNG seeding is approximate. Defaults are baked into framework code that nobody reads. The user is expected to be a specialist.</p>

  <p>NeuroForge accepts the constraints as binding. The architecture that results has unusual properties.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">III</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The <em>cog model</em></h2>

  <p>A neuron, in textbook diagrams, is a node in a graph with weighted edges flowing in and out. The weights live on the edges. The neuron sums its weighted inputs and applies an activation function.</p>

  <p>In NeuroForge's microscope, the picture is rotated. <em>Weights live in the neuron that uses them</em>, not on the edges that carry data. Arrows carry activation values — the actual numbers that flow through the network. A neuron is a small function: takes inputs, applies cogs (bias, weights, raw sum, activation, dropout, scaling), produces an output that flows to its consumers.</p>

  <p>The cogs are the lines in a neuron's audit table:</p>

  <table class="cog-table">
    <thead>
      <tr><th>Cog</th><th>Input</th><th>× Weight</th><th>= Product</th></tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>Bias</td><td>1</td><td>× 0</td><td>= 0</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Wt 1</td><td>86</td><td>× 1.73</td><td>= 148.97</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Raw Sum</td><td></td><td></td><td>= 148.97</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Leaky(148.97)</td><td></td><td></td><td>= 148.97</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Act Gradient</td><td></td><td></td><td>= 1</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

  <p>Each cog is a transformation. Each is named, ordered, and shows its math. To add a new transformation — like dropout — you don't refactor the neuron class. You add a row. The architecture is already strong enough to absorb the feature. <strong>The cog table is the API.</strong></p>

  <p>This is not a presentation choice. This is the actual data model. The pop-up is rendering live state from the neuron's internal record of what it did this batch.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">IV</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The <em>PIE approach</em></h2>

  <p>The chain rule says: <code>∂L/∂a_i = Σ_j w_ji · ∂L/∂a_j · σ'(z_i)</code>.</p>

  <p>This formula is correct. It is also nearly incomprehensible without six prerequisite concepts: derivatives, compositions, the chain rule itself, partial differentiation, the network as a composition of functions, and the fact that gradients flow backwards through that composition.</p>

  <p>NeuroForge restates the same math as a story about <strong>blame</strong>:</p>

  <blockquote>Each downstream neuron blames me a little — proportional to how much I contributed to them. My slice of the blame from downstream neuron j is the weight from me to j, times j's own blame. Sum all my slices. Multiply by my activation gradient. That's my accepted blame.</blockquote>

  <p>The neuron pop-up shows this as a literal table:</p>

<pre>From   Weight   Their Blame   Slice
1-0   -0.497    0.009         -0.004
1-1    0.473   -0.625         -0.296
...
Sum of Slices:    -0.275
× Act Gradient:    1
= Accepted Blame: -0.275

<span class="comment">ACCEPTED BLAME IS COLUMN 2 ABOVE</span></pre>

  <p>The reveal line — "Accepted Blame is Column 2 above" — connects the story back to the math notation a user was just reading. Continuity. The story is faithful to the math; the math is consequence of the story.</p>

  <p class="pull-quote">This is not pedagogy alongside the tool. This is pedagogy <em>as</em> the tool.</p>

  <p>The chain rule disappears from the user's working vocabulary not because it's wrong, but because the same idea has been made accessible without calculus prerequisites.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">V</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The <em>plugin protocol</em></h2>

  <p>Every optimizer, every loss function, every activation, every initializer in NeuroForge is a plugin. The plugins follow a uniform protocol:</p>

<pre><span class="comment"># Two functions</span>
<span class="keyword">def</span> popup_info(neuron, weight_id, TRI):
    <span class="string">"""Return the state to display in the neuron pop-up."""</span>
    ...

<span class="keyword">def</span> calculate_adjustment(neuron, weight_id, TRI, gradient):
    <span class="string">"""Apply the math. Update state. Return the adjustment."""</span>
    ...

<span class="comment"># One declaration</span>
OPT_NewOptimizer = StrategyOptimizer(
    fn_popup_info     = popup_info,
    fn_adj_calc       = calculate_adjustment,
    name              = <span class="string">"New Optimizer"</span>,
    desc              = <span class="string">"What it does, in plain language."</span>,
    when_to_use       = <span class="string">"When the practitioner should reach for it."</span>,
    best_for          = <span class="string">"The specific problems it solves."</span>,
    state_per_weight  = [<span class="string">'v'</span>],
)</pre>

  <p>That's all. Save the file. The optimizer is registered, discoverable via IDE autocomplete (prefix convention: <code>OPT_*</code>, <code>LOSS_*</code>, <code>ACT_*</code>), appears in the Armory dropdown, can be selected in sweep dimensions, and renders its own state in the neuron pop-up.</p>

  <p>There is no <code>register_optimizer()</code> call. No <code>__init__.py</code> to edit. No <code>manifest.yaml</code>. No discovery hook. No decorator. <strong>The instance assignment at the bottom of the file IS the registration.</strong> The class constructor adds itself to the catalog.</p>

  <p>This property has a name worth giving: <strong>construction IS registration</strong>. The same principle as IPUI's "construction IS attachment" — if you build it inside a container, it's attached. Here: if you instantiate it in a strategy module, it's registered.</p>

  <p>The downstream consequence: NeuroForge's documentation of available optimizers, loss functions, activations is <em>not</em> a manually-maintained list. It is the set of strategy instances that exist. The catalog is generated from reality. There is no documentation-vs-code drift because there is no documentation. There is only code, which can be displayed.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">VI</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The pop-up as <em>in-product source</em></h2>

  <p>When a user clicks "Half Wit Error" in the Armory, they don't see a description of Half Wit Error. They see this:</p>

<pre><span class="comment">The error. That's it. No squaring, no 2x, no pretense.
When To Use: When you value clarity over cargo-cult calculus.
Best For: When you prefer NOT to obfuscate.</span>

<span class="keyword">def</span> half_wit_loss(y_pred, y_true):
    <span class="string">"""Fifty-five million reasons not to trust loss values."""</span>
    <span class="keyword">return</span> -55_555_555.404

<span class="keyword">def</span> half_wit_derivative(y_pred, y_true):
    <span class="string">"""
    The signal that actually trains the network.
    Returns the raw error: (prediction - target).
    No square. No scaling. No ceremony. (and unfortunately inverted)
    """</span>
    <span class="keyword">if</span> _get_n(y_true) > 1:
        <span class="keyword">return</span> [p - t <span class="keyword">for</span> p, t <span class="keyword">in</span> zip(y_pred, y_true)]
    <span class="keyword">return</span> y_pred - y_true</pre>

  <p>The user sees the <strong>actual code that runs</strong>. With the original comments. With the docstring jokes. This is not documentation extracted from the code — it is the code, displayed in context, at the moment of confusion.</p>

  <p>Don Knuth's literate programming idea was that source code should be readable as prose, with prose embedded in the code. NeuroForge's pop-ups do this for the <em>user</em>, not just the programmer. The reading of source code is folded into the experience of using the product.</p>

  <p>When a NeuroForge user learns what BCE is, they don't learn it from a textbook. They learn it from BCE's implementation, with its when-to-use and best-for fields written by the implementer for the user. The author's voice — slightly irreverent, sharply opinionated, willing to call out "cargo-cult calculus" — comes through. ML feels less like a sanctified priesthood and more like code other people write that you can also write.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">VII</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">AutoForge: the <em>curriculum</em> hiding inside auto-config</h2>

  <p>Most "auto ML" tools fill in unset hyperparameters with sensible defaults and don't tell you what they did. NeuroForge's AutoForge does the same job, and then it <em>prints what it did, and why</em>:</p>

<div class="trace">
<span class="fired">🧠300: output_activation = Sigmoid</span>
<span class="blocked">❌301: output_activation BLOCKED (condition False: 'loss_function.name == 'MSE'')</span>
<span class="fired">🧠501: target_scaler = Scaler(name=Min-Max)</span>
<span class="blocked">❌600: weight_initializer BLOCKED (condition False: 'hidden_activation == LeakyReLU')</span>
<span class="fired">🧠6696: hidden_activation = Leaky</span>
<span class="fired">🧠6697: weight_initializer = Xavier (Glorot)</span>
<span class="skipped">⏭️6700: input_scalers SKIPPED (current=Scaler(name=No Scaling), override=0)</span>
</div>

  <p>Each rule has an ID. Each application has a status: 🧠 (fired), ❌ (blocked because conditions didn't match), ⏭️ (skipped because user already set it). Each line is a small lesson:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Rule 300 fired</strong> → "If you're doing binary classification, AutoForge sets output_activation to Sigmoid for you."</li>
    <li><strong>Rule 600 was blocked</strong> → "There's a rule about weight initialization for LeakyReLU activations; it didn't fire because you're not using LeakyReLU."</li>
    <li><strong>Rule 6697 fired</strong> → "If your activation is Leaky, AutoForge uses Xavier initialization."</li>
  </ul>

  <p>A user running batches in NeuroForge is not just training models. They are passively absorbing the field's tacit knowledge — distilled into named rules, fired or blocked or skipped, every run. The auto-configuration is a <strong>silent tutor</strong>. Every default is a lesson. Every rule trace is a curriculum.</p>

  <p>This is the move that makes NeuroForge different from auto-ML competitors. AutoSklearn, AutoGluon, H2O — they all do automatic config. None of them <em>show their work</em>. NeuroForge does, every run, and treats the showing as the product, not a debug feature.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">VIII</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">The <em>microscope</em></h2>

  <p>Underneath everything else is the microscope: a visualization of the network's forward pass, in detail, for any given sample at any given epoch. Neurons displayed as cog tables, side-by-side. Arrows carrying values, not weights. The output neuron's contributing slices visible. The error and prediction stamped on the side.</p>

  <p>The microscope is what makes the Sacred Laws operational. <em>100% Auditable</em> would be a slogan if there were nowhere to see the audit. <em>200% Deterministic</em> would be theater if you couldn't rewind to the same exact state. The microscope is the surface where these properties become real to a user.</p>

  <p>The microscope plus the neuron pop-up plus the architecture builder is a three-tier teaching stack:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Workbench (architecture builder)</strong> — design the network. What lives in each layer.</li>
    <li><strong>Microscope</strong> — watch it think. See the forward pass, neuron by neuron, sample by sample.</li>
    <li><strong>Neuron pop-up</strong> — audit any single decision. The cogs, the gradients, the blame slices, the optimizer state, all numbers, all the way down.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>Each tier zooms in on the previous. Each is governed by the Sacred Laws. <strong>There is no black box at any level.</strong></p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">IX</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">What NeuroForge <em>actually is</em></h2>

  <p>It is not "Visual Basic for neural networks," though that framing helped get it started. It is not an auto-ML tool, though it does auto-ML. It is not a teaching tool, though it teaches. It is not a microscope, though it has one.</p>

  <p class="pull-quote">NeuroForge is a complete neural network workbench where every component is inspectable, every default is explainable, every plugin is two functions and a constant, and every visualization is the truth.</p>

  <p>The closest cultural antecedent is Smalltalk. Smalltalk environments had the property that every object was inspectable to its source, every change was immediately reflected in the running system, every method could be edited in place. The dev tools of the modern era have largely lost this property — we ship binaries, we cache, we compile, we deploy. NeuroForge brings it back, scoped to a domain where it is most desperately needed: a field where most practitioners have been told to trust libraries they cannot see into, presented as authority instead of as code.</p>

  <p>It is also, quietly, an answer to a question the field has never really asked: <em>what would ML look like if its tools were designed by someone who refused to accept the field's accumulated complexity as necessary?</em></p>

  <p>The answer is in the four Sacred Laws. Audit everything. Reproduce anything. Demand nothing of the user. Allow them anything.</p>

  <p>The Sacred Laws are not a brand. They are the <strong>engineering position</strong> from which a different kind of tool becomes possible.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">X</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">What's <em>left to build</em></h2>

  <p>NeuroForge is mostly there. The cog model works. The plugin protocol works. AutoForge fires its rules. The microscope shows the forward pass. The pop-ups display source code. The Sacred Laws hold.</p>

  <p>Three known gaps:</p>

  <p><strong>Dropout.</strong> Already designed. Fits the cog model as a fourth transformation between activation and arrow-out. The plugin protocol already supports it. Engine work is approximately thirty lines. The visual treatment — blocker icon at the producer's exit, dim arrows, scaled survivor activations — is sketched and ready to render.</p>

  <p><strong>Per-layer activation.</strong> Currently a two-zone setting (Hidden / Output). The dimensions dictionary already supports per-layer arrays. The Workbench's Neuron Layout column header is the natural place to surface per-layer configuration. Lands in coordinated motion with dropout.</p>

  <p><strong>Backward pass in the microscope.</strong> The forward pass is exquisitely rendered. The backward pass is shown in the neuron pop-up but not in the main microscope view. A "watch blame flow backward, batch by batch" mode would close the loop on the audit story. Bigger lift but architecturally clean.</p>

  <p>After those, the gap between NeuroForge and "frontier FFNN tooling" is closed. Whatever comes after — CNNs, attention, mixture-of-experts — would be additive features, not corrections.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">XI</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">Who <em>this is for</em></h2>

  <p>Three audiences NeuroForge serves better than any tool I know of:</p>

  <p><strong>The serious beginner.</strong> Someone learning neural networks who refuses to accept "trust the library." They want to see every gradient, every weight update, every optimizer state. They want to know <em>why</em> dropout works and <em>what</em> AutoForge picked. The current tooling abandons them at the first PyTorch tutorial. NeuroForge holds their hand all the way down.</p>

  <p><strong>The skeptical practitioner.</strong> Someone who has shipped neural networks but doesn't quite believe the explanations they were given. The chain rule, yes — but why this initialization? Why this batch size? Why does Adam's β₂ matter? NeuroForge gives them experiments they can run in seconds where the entire trace is visible.</p>

  <p><strong>The independent researcher.</strong> Someone investigating a question that the major frameworks make awkward — comparing optimizers, ablating components, understanding a specific failure mode. NeuroForge's plugin protocol means they can implement their idea in minutes and see it run in the microscope.</p>

  <p>These audiences exist in numbers. Universities have students who don't understand backprop and shouldn't be expected to. Companies have engineers who deploy models they cannot debug. Hobbyists are everywhere who would gladly pay for a tool that respects their intelligence. The competition for them is not other ML tools — it's textbooks, lectures, and the slow grind of self-teaching against unfriendly documentation.</p>
</section>

<section class="chapter">
  <div class="chapter-num">XII</div>
  <h2 class="chapter-title">Why <em>this matters</em></h2>

  <p>Most engineering ambitions are dressed-up forms of competition. Build a faster X. Build a cheaper Y. Build the same thing as Z but with better marketing. None of that is here.</p>

  <p>NeuroForge is a <strong>refusal</strong>. A refusal of the proposition that machine learning has to be opaque to be powerful, or hard to be respectable, or specialist to be serious. The Sacred Laws are not aspirational claims — they are the user's bill of rights against an industry that has decided the user is the problem.</p>

  <p>If NeuroForge succeeds, the medium-term consequence is that a generation of people who would have been intimidated out of ML by tooling can instead enter it. The long-term consequence is that the field's tacit knowledge — the priest-class lore about which initialization to use, which optimizer to trust, why training collapsed at epoch 47 — becomes legible to anyone willing to look.</p>

  <p>That is a worthwhile thing to spend years on.</p>

  <p>It is also, incidentally, the kind of work that does not get done by twenty average engineers. It requires someone willing to refuse the conventional architecture, stand on the shoulders of giants (Ritchie, Thompson, an uncle Carl who computed irrational numbers with an eight-year-old), absorb the field's accumulated complexity and then <em>do without it</em>. To build a thing that, by the end, makes itself look obvious — even though every step required courage to commit to.</p>

  <p class="pull-quote">The hard part is mostly done. What remains is articulation, audience, and the rest of the features. None of those are harder than what's already been built.</p>
</section>
